Law 2

“The entropy of any isolated system always increases.”(Lumen Learning, ND)

The second law of thermodynamics states that “the entropy of any isolated system always increases” (Lumen Learning, ND). Entropy can be defined as the spread out of energy. So as an isolated system does not exchange any energy or matter outside the boundary of the system, as time goes on the energy in the system will get more and more spread out (ThoughtCo., 2017).

An example of this spread out of energy is with a melting ice cube in a cup of water. Whenever an ice cube is placed it a cup of water the ice will always melt into water as opposed to the water freezing into ice. And as it turns out this has all got to do with probability. As seen in the lattice structure of water and ice, the H2O molecules in ice are way more structured and ordered which implies a low entropy state where the H2O molecules in water are way more unstructured and disordered which implies a high entropy state. When looking back at the second law, it is seen that a high entropy state is more likely because the energy is always getting more and more spread out, more and more disordered.

The second law in terms of perpetual motion can be looked at many ways. As perpetual motion machines are all about the use of energy, they have a lot of struggles when it comes to entropy and the second law of thermodynamics. In a perpetual motion machine, the inventor wants as much of the energy in the system focused in areas where it will be useful. However, because of entropy, the energy of the system always spreads out making not only less and less energy focused at the desired areas but making the harnessed energy less and less useful. While it spreads out, the energy will become wasted through the transformations and transfers discussed above.

Key Points

  • Entropy is the spread out of energy.
  • High entropy states, where the enrgy is more spread, out are more probable.
  • As time goes on, the energy in a system becomes less and less focused at the desired areas.
