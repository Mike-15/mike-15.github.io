Battle Equipment

Equipment used by Australian soldiers in the Battle Field

Rifle

Issued: 1914 (Start of War)

A bolt action rifle was the primary gun used by Australians and the British during the war and one was given to every soldier. The rifle also included a webbing strap so soldiers didn't have to hold it when not in use. The rifles used were all manufactured in Britain as the Australian manufacture coould not meet the demand for rifles. The rifle used 56mm Mark VI ball ammunition and later 56mm Mark VII ball ammunition.

Bayonet

Issued: 1914 (Start of War)

A bayonet was issued to every soldier. It would atatch to the front of the rifle incase soldiers ever needed to do any hand in hand combat. The first version included a hook on the handle so they coulf hold it easily in their hand. This was later removed. The bayonet was not always used so their was a piece of carrier webbing so it could be held on the main piece of webbing.

Ammunition Pouches

Issued: 1914 (Start of War)

Across the chest of the 1908 Webbing there was 10 pouches that could hold up to 150 rounds of ammunition. This was very important as the soldier used his rifle alot and would need a suffient amount of ammunition if moving in and out of the trenches.


The picture (right) shows half of the pouches. Each individual pouch held 15 rounds of ammunition.

2 Part Entrenching Tool

Issued: 1914 (Start of War)

The entrenching tool came in 2 parts, the head (helve) which was stored in a pouch at the rear of the webbing and the wooden handle which was stored next to the bayonet. Soldiers manly used the to dig trenches but became very handy to dig a small hole if they were getting shot at. The helve was made out of cast iron.

Water Bottle with Carrier Webbing

Issued: 1914 (Start of War)

The one litere water bottle was an important piece of kit as water was very limited. It included a felt insulation around the outside and a piece of carrier webbing to atatch to the main piece of webbing. The insulation helped keep the water fresh but soldiers had to drink it quickly otherwise it would go brown and yucky.

Emergency Rations

Issued: 1914 (Start of War)

The emergency rations kit included a fork, spoon and tinned food. Most of the smaller items like this, spare clothing and more would be stored in a pack (haversack) on the back of the webbing.

Kindling

Issued: 1914 (Start of War)

A bundle of wood tied together with some yarn was given to soldiers at the start of war, it was later taken of the list of official kit after Gallipoli. Soldiers used this to make fires as the winter months provided very cold weather.

Spare Clothing

Issued: 1914 (Start of War)

In battle arrangment soldiers would be found with a couple of spare t-shirts, socks and underpants.

Steel Helmet

Issued: 1916 (Middle of War)

From 1916 onwards there were heaps of designs and variations of the steel helmet but the most common was the 'Brodie Mark 1'. Helmets were brought into the war to help stop injuries from exploding shrapnal as grenades were also a common weapon.


The helmet was entirely steel with an adjustable leather chin strap and some foam padding on the inside for comfort.

Gas Mask

Issued: 1916 (Middle of War)

Before the war Country representitives gathered to discuss 'the rules of war'. What they came up with was the 'Geneva Protocol' which banned the use of chemical weapons in warfar. However this rule heavely broken during the middle of WW1, first by the Germans. Chlorine and Musdard gas caused many deaths but just as many injuries.


To fight against this weapon, in 1916, gas masks and respirators were issued as an offial piece of kit. As well as for themselves, soldiers with animal companions were also responisble for puting a mask on their pet. These gas masks did save lives but they were very uncomfortable and often left soldiers feeling sick and tired.
